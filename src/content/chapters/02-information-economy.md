## Information Logistics (Augustin, 1990)

The **Information Economy** is the demand-side layer of the Krcmar model. Its core problem: organizations have data, but decision-makers need **information** — relevant, timely, and contextualized.

> **Definition:** The **logistical principle** (Augustin, 1990) states that we must supply the **right information**, at the **right time**, in the **right amount**, at the **right place**, in the **required quality**.

## Eliciting Information Demand

Three sub-steps: **identify stakeholders and personas**, **elicit people's needs** (subjective), and **understand company needs** (objective).

### Picot's Three Dimensions (Picot, 1988)

**Information Demand** = the intersection of three dimensions: Objective Need (what's necessary for the task), Subjective Need (what's perceived necessary), and Available Information (what exists).

The gaps between dimensions have specific meanings:

| Gap | Meaning | Implication |
|-----|---------|-------------|
| Objective but **not** Subjective | Person doesn't realize they need it | **Training gap** |
| Subjective but **not** Objective | Person thinks they need it, but it's not task-relevant | **Noise** / information overload |
| Needed but **not** Available | Information doesn't exist or isn't accessible | **Information gap** — needs new sources |
| Available but **not** Needed | Exists but no one requires it | **Overload risk** |

| Approach | Method | Examples |
|----------|--------|----------|
| **Subjective** | Derive from personal interpretation | Interviews, wishlists |
| **Objective** | Derive from inter-subjectively validated interpretation | Strategic analysis, process analysis |
| **Mixed** | Objective first, then subjective | Process analysis → interviews |

### Stakeholders and Personas

> **Definition:** A **stakeholder** is a person or organization who influences a system's requirements or who is impacted by that system. Influence can also be indirect (Glinz, 2020).

| Level | Stakeholder Types | Information Focus |
|-------|------------------|-------------------|
| **Strategic** | Senior management | Strategic direction, high-level KPIs |
| **Tactical** | Middle management, knowledge workers | Departmental performance, resource allocation |
| **Operational** | Operational managers, data workers | Day-to-day operations, task instructions |

> **Important:** Different stakeholders view the same system from entirely different perspectives. Accounting for all views is essential for complete requirements.

**Personas** are fictional individuals representing a user group, built from empirical data (interviews, analytics). Each captures demographics, goals, frustrations, and motivations — providing concrete guidance instead of designing for a vague user.

### Information Quality

| Contextual | Presentational | Intrinsic | Access |
|------------|---------------|-----------|--------|
| Currentness | Understandability | Reliability | Accessibility |
| Completeness | Clarity | Correctness | Maintainability |
| Appropriate Scope | Uniformity | Objectivity | |
| Relevance | Unambiguity | Credibility | |

> **Important:** Even correct information can fail if it is not current, accessible, or understandable. All four quality dimensions must be considered.

### Sources vs. Resources

An **information source** is any origin of data (database, report, person). When a source is **used recurrently** and managed for reuse, it becomes an **information resource**. Sources are discovered; resources are *engineered* — organized, indexed, and maintained for efficient reuse.

### Organizing Resources

| Approach | Structure | Examples |
|----------|-----------|----------|
| **Hierarchies** | Tree-structured classification | Taxonomy |
| **Keywords** | Flat or semi-structured labels | Thesaurus, Tags |
| **Graphs** | Network of linked concepts | Semantic Web |

### Taxonomies

A **taxonomy** is a hierarchical classification: a set of **labels** L and **relationships** R where r = ⟨l₁, l₂⟩. Labels are either **instances** (specific individuals) or **classes** (categories), with relationships being *is-a* or *member-of* accordingly.

> **Important:** Taxonomy relationships can form directed acyclic graphs (not just strict trees) — a label can appear under multiple parents.

### The Semantic Web (Tim Berners-Lee)

The **Semantic Web** is the graph-based approach: machines understand *meaning*, not just syntax. Architecture layers: **URI/Unicode** (identifiers) → **XML** (syntax) → **RDF triples** (subject–predicate–object data model) → **Ontologies** (shared vocabularies). For IM, it enables **cross-system linking** through shared ontologies, reducing data silos.

| Stage | Process | Purpose |
|-------|---------|---------|
| **Extraction** | Pull data from multiple heterogeneous source systems | Gather raw operational data |
| **Transformation** | Clean, standardize, and restructure the data | Ensure consistency and quality |
| **Loading** | Store the processed data in the warehouse | Make it available for analysis |

**Transformation** is the most complex ETL step: **data cleaning** (fixing errors, missing values), **deduplication**, **format standardization** (dates, units, encodings), **business rule application** (derived fields), and **schema mapping** (aligning source schemas to target). Without proper transformation, the warehouse inherits source system inconsistencies.
